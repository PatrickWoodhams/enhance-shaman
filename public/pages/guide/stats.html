Mental models, not myths

Stats Explained

This page teaches you how Enhancement stats actually work in TBC, so you can stop guessing. You will learn what each stat changes, why EP is context dependent, how caps change value, and exactly when you should sim instead of relying on rules of thumb.

How to use this stats guide

Read this page once to build your mental model, then use it as a reference when you are comparing upgrades. The goal is not to memorize a single stat priority line. The goal is to understand what changes when you add or remove a stat, and when that change becomes small.

Learn what each stat controls
Start with the legend and the stat cards. You should be able to describe each stat in one sentence.
Understand EP and why it moves
EP is a tool to compare tradeoffs. It changes with your weapons, buffs, caps, and rotation choices.
When the decision is close, sim
If you cannot confidently explain the trade, you should sim the exact items in your current context.
Rule: If the upgrade is not obvious, sim. If you are still unsure after simming, you likely missed an input.

This page is intentionally thorough. It is written to make you consistent, not to make you feel clever.

Stat Breakdown

Each card gives you a usable mental model for what the stat is really buying, plus the common traps that create bad upgrade decisions. This version also includes quick conversions so you can translate item text into real outcomes. Use these numbers to sanity check upgrades, then sim when choices are close or your setup changes.

Kings: no change

Base: 22.1 rating = 1 percent crit
Kings: no change

Kings: no change

Expertise
Base: 3.94 rating = 1 Expertise
Kings: no change

Intellect
Base: 1 Intellect = 15 mana
Kings: 1 Intellect = 16.5 mana
 Use: rotation comfort and fewer dead moments. Buff note: Kings increases Intellect, which increases your mana pool. Extra note: Intellect also increases spell crit. Rough rule: about 80 Intellect is 1 percent spell crit.
Mana per 5
Base: 1 Mp5 = 1 mana every 5 seconds
Kings: no change
 Use: steady sustain that supports aggressive shock use and fewer pauses. Watch for: Mp5 value rises on longer fights and in groups with fewer external mana tools.
Spell Power
Base: adds damage to spells
Kings: no change

Stamina
Base: 1 Stamina = 10 health
Kings: 1 Stamina = 11 health

Weapon DPS and speed

Your biggest lever. Always sim close weapon swaps.
  • What it does: Weapon DPS raises your baseline damage, which everything else multiplies. Weapon speed influences swing timing, proc behavior, and how your rotation fits between swings.
  • Quick conversion: Attack Power adds weapon damage at (Weapon Speed × Attack Power ÷ 14) per swing. This is why AP and weapon speed both matter when you feel a weapon upgrade.
  • Why you care: A small DPS increase on a weapon often beats a large pile of stats on other slots because the whole kit scales off your weapons.
  • Common trap: Equipping a faster weapon because the secondary stats look better, then losing Windfury value and messing up timing and consistency. When weapons are close, sim it instead of guessing.

Hit Rating

Prevents misses. Strong when you are losing key swings.
  • What it does: Reduces the chance your physical attacks miss. A missed swing is not just lost damage, it is also a lost chance to proc effects and a lost chance to crit.
  • Conversion: At level 70, 15.8 Hit Rating is about 1 percent melee hit.
  • Boss context: Against level 73 bosses, special attacks start at 9 percent base miss. Dual wield auto swings have an additional miss penalty, so fully capping white swings is usually not the goal.
  • When it is strongest: When you are missing important attacks. Early on, each point of hit often creates a noticeable stability gain because fewer swings are completely wasted.
  • When it collapses: When you are already covering the thing you are trying to protect for your current content and rotation. Past that point, hit stops preventing failure and becomes just another small damage stat.
  • Common trap: Downgrading a major weapon or Strength upgrade just to chase a cap number that does not improve your real results in that gear set.

Expertise

Prevents dodges. Extremely valuable until covered.
  • What it does: Reduces the chance your attacks are dodged and parried. From behind, bosses cannot parry you, so the practical value is mostly about removing dodges.
  • Conversion: About 3.94 Expertise Rating gives 1 Expertise. Each point reduces dodge and parry chance by 0.25 percent.
  • Why it is strong: Turning dodges into hits increases damage and smooths your proc and crit flow. It often feels like your whole rotation gets more reliable.
  • Common trap: Treating Expertise like a permanent top stat even after you have effectively removed most dodge value for your situation. Once it stops preventing dodges, its value drops sharply.

Strength and Attack Power

Core physical scaling. Usually your safest damage stat.
  • What it does: Strength converts into Attack Power and scales your physical damage. It is simple, consistent value that keeps working in every gear set.
  • Conversion: 1 Strength = 2 Attack Power. Rule of thumb: 14 Attack Power = 1 weapon DPS, which is why Strength and AP remain valuable even when caps are handled.
  • Why it stays stable: Unlike Hit or Expertise, it does not turn off when you pass a threshold. It keeps adding damage even when other stats start to lose efficiency.
  • Common trap: Forgetting buffs. Kings increases Strength and Agility, which can shift the best choice between items that look close.

Crit Rating and Agility

Supports Flurry uptime and burst windows.
  • What it does: Increases crit chance, improving burst and reinforcing effects that care about crits, including keeping Flurry rolling.
  • Conversions: At level 70, 22.1 Crit Rating is about 1 percent crit. For Shaman, 1 Agility gives about 0.04 percent crit and also adds dodge and armor.
  • When it is better than it looks: When your swings are connecting reliably. Crit feeds Flurry, and Flurry increases the number of future swings that can also crit.
  • Common trap: Comparing crit numbers without considering how many swings are actually connecting. Misses and dodges reduce the number of chances you even get to roll a crit.

Haste Rating

More swings, more procs, more scaling when your swings land.
  • What it does: Increases swing frequency. More swings means more white damage and more chances for on hit effects to occur.
  • Conversion: At level 70, 15.8 Haste Rating is about 1 percent haste.
  • When it is stronger: When your swings are consistently landing. Haste multiplies whatever you are already doing well.
  • Common trap: Stacking haste while missing or getting dodged, then blaming haste. If swings do not land, haste just creates more failed swings.

Armor Penetration

Physical gain that shows up later. Usually not a gem priority early.
  • What it does: Lets your physical attacks ignore some armor, increasing the portion of your damage that gets through.
  • Conversion note: There is no single universal percent conversion that stays true across bosses, because the gain depends on the target armor and your damage mix. Treat this as a sim stat once you are past basic reliability needs.
  • When it shines: Later gear sets, when your baseline weapon damage and Attack Power are already high enough that reducing armor meaningfully amplifies that damage.
  • Common trap: Chasing Armor Pen early at the cost of stats that prevent failures like Hit or Expertise, or giving up major Strength and weapon upgrades.

Spell hit, spell power, and mana stats

Not primary, but not irrelevant. Often decides comfort and consistency.
  • Spell Hit Rating: Impacts shocks and other spell events. In TBC, Hit Rating and Spell Hit Rating are separate, so your physical hit does not automatically fix missed shocks.
  • Conversion: At level 70, 12.6 Spell Hit Rating is about 1 percent spell hit.
  • Spell Power: Improves shocks and other magic damage sources. It is usually secondary to weapon and physical scaling, but it still contributes, especially when you are casting on cooldown.
  • Intellect and Mana per 5: Intellect is 15 mana per point and also increases spell crit. If you run out of mana, your real DPS falls off a cliff no matter how strong your gear looks on paper. Mana per 5 is pure sustain and is often the difference between keeping the rotation aggressive or having to back off.
  • Stamina: Typically 10 health per point. Surviving mechanics preserves uptime and lets you keep attacking instead of playing scared.
Stat weights

EP and stat weights

EP helps you compare two items faster by converting different stats into one number. It is useful for quick decisions, but the numbers are only true for your exact setup.

EP is personal: it changes with your gear, talents, buffs, debuffs, and rotation.
EP is not a rule: do not treat it like a permanent stat priority list.

The one sentence definition

Pick a baseline (usually 1 Attack Power), then write every other stat as “how much Attack Power it is worth” for your current character and raid setup.

A simple example

If 1 Strength = 2.20 EP, then in that setup 1 Strength gives about the same damage value as 2.20 Attack Power. If your hit, expertise, buffs, or weapons change, that number can move.

How to use EP without getting baited

  • Use EP to triage. Filter obvious upgrades and obvious downgrades quickly.
  • Sim close calls. If two items look close, run a sim with your exact gear and buffs.
  • Respect caps. Hit and Expertise can drop in value fast once you are near or past the cap that matters for that boss.

Breakpoints

Caps, attack tables, and why value collapses

A cap is not a goal by itself. A cap is the point where extra rating stops removing real failures in your combat log. When the failure is already rare, the stat stops buying new successful actions, so its EP drops hard.

The model that explains everything

Use this for every upgrade
  1. Name the failure. Specials missing. White swings missing. Shocks missing. Mana collapse. Timing issues.
  2. Pick the stat that removes it. Hit removes melee misses. Spell hit removes spell misses. Throughput stats multiply your output once actions are landing reliably.
  3. Stop paying once the failure is mostly gone. Near a cap there are fewer failures left to convert, so the same rating produces fewer real gains.

Why EP collapses near caps

EP measures how many new successful actions a stat creates in your current setup. Early on, hit converts many misses into hits. Near a cap, there are not many misses left, so extra hit creates fewer new landed actions over the same time window. That is the value collapse.

Enhancement rule: Make your core abilities land reliably first. Then build throughput with the best stat mix for your gear, buffs, and encounter.

Enhancement priorities vs a raid boss

1

Special hit first

Your first job is to stop missing key abilities. Missed Stormstrike means lost damage and fewer chances to trigger your core engines.

  • What you are fixing: special attack misses against a level 73 boss
  • Baseline: specials miss chance is 9 percent vs a boss
  • Enhancement default: Dual Wield Specialization gives 6 percent hit, so the common target is about 3 percent hit from gear or Nature's Guidance
  • Reality check: raid and party effects can change the exact number, so validate using your character sheet and logs
2

White swing consistency still matters

Dual wield white miss stays high. Even after specials are stable, hit can still add value by increasing landed swings, Windfury chances, and Flurry stability.

  • Key idea: you are not chasing a white hit cap in normal TBC Enhancement gearing
  • How to use this: after special hit is stable, treat hit like any other stat and let your sim decide if it beats haste, crit, and strength in your current setup
3

Spell hit is situational

Shocks use spell hit. The boss spell miss pool is large, so Enhancement typically does not chase a full spell hit cap. Spell hit matters when shock misses are a real problem and you are actually playing a shock heavy style.

  • Default: do not chase full spell hit as a baseline goal
  • When it matters: if shock misses are frequent in logs and you have the mana and globals to support shocks without breaking the rest of your kit

Quick conversions at level 70

  • 15.77 melee hit rating ≈ 1 percent melee hit
  • 12.62 spell hit rating ≈ 1 percent spell hit
  • 22.08 crit rating ≈ 1 percent crit
  • 15.77 haste rating ≈ 1 percent haste
  • 3.94 expertise rating ≈ 1 expertise (bonus if present, not a target)

Attack tables, in plain English

A melee swing rolls against an outcome table. Miss and dodge happen before crit is even considered. If miss is high, many swings fail before they ever get a chance to be a crit. Hit does not increase crit chance. Hit increases the number of swings that land, which increases the number of chances you have to roll crits, and that stabilizes Flurry and proc flow over time.

What “value collapse” looks like

When you are missing often, 1 percent hit converts a large number of misses into hits. Once specials rarely miss, the same 1 percent hit converts very few misses. That is why hit EP can drop sharply right after you reach reliable special hit.

Expertise, the Enhancement reality

Expertise reduces dodge. That is mechanically good, but TBC has very little Enhancement friendly expertise itemization, and it competes with stronger stat packages. Treat expertise as a bonus when it appears. Do not plan your gearing around an expertise cap.

Bottom line: the breakpoint you plan around is special hit reliability. After that, use sims to choose the best throughput mix for your gear and raid context.
Buffs

Raid buffs, raid debuffs, and what they change

Enhancement gearing does not happen in a vacuum. In a real raid, your stats are being increased by raid buffs on you, your party is being boosted by group limited effects, and the boss is being weakened by target debuffs. Those layers change what each stat actually delivers.

How to read this section: each line has an Effect and a Why it matters. If you are comparing two items and your raid coverage changes, your “best stat” list can change with it.

Raid buffs on you

These are the buffs that most commonly shift Enhancement item comparisons, especially when you are close to hit or expertise breakpoints.

Base stats and percent scaling

These change the real value of Strength, Agility, Intellect, and Stamina
  • Paladin Raidwide
    Effect: Increases your base attributes by a percent.
    Why it matters: This multiplies what you already have, including stats gained from gear. A +10 Strength upgrade is not just +10 Strength in a Kings raid. It is +10 Strength, then multiplied. That is why “Strength and Agility get better in raids” is often literally true.
  • Druid Raidwide
    Effect: Adds a flat amount of base stats (plus defensive baseline).
    Why it matters: This raises your baseline before percent multipliers apply. Mark plus Kings is not redundant. Mark increases the base, Kings multiplies it. Together they meaningfully change the “real” amount of Strength and Agility behind your Attack Power and crit.
  • Priest Raidwide
    Effect: Adds Stamina.
    Why it matters: Stamina does not raise your damage directly, but it raises your chance to keep hitting the boss. If an avoidable death or a healer panic forces you out of melee range, your DPS drops to zero. Fortitude improves your ability to stay in, keep Flurry rolling, and not lose uptime during damage spikes.
  • Mage Raidwide
    Effect: Adds Intellect, increasing mana pool and slightly increasing spell crit.
    Why it matters: A bigger mana pool gives you more “rotation budget” before you have to throttle shocks, purge, grounding, or utility casts. Intellect rarely wins gear decisions by itself, but it changes how long you can maintain your preferred play pattern on long fights.
  • Priest Raidwide
    Effect: Adds Spirit, improving mana regeneration when you are not actively spending mana.
    Why it matters: Spirit is not a burst stat for Enhancement, but it is a stability stat. It helps between pulls, during movement, and anytime you temporarily ease off shocks. If your raid has downtime patterns, Spirit buffs reduce the chance you return to melee already behind on mana.
Gearing takeaway: In raids with Kings plus Mark, Strength and Agility upgrades are worth more than they look in isolation because your baseline attributes are higher and percent scaling is active.

Attack Power and melee throughput

Flat Attack Power plus percent multipliers that boost your whole physical kit
  • Paladin Raidwide
    Effect: Adds flat Attack Power.
    Why it matters: Flat AP increases your white hits and many physical abilities. It also increases the payoff of percent multipliers and target debuffs, because those multipliers are being applied to a larger physical baseline.
  • Warrior Party
    Effect: Adds flat Attack Power to the group.
    Why it matters: When Battle Shout is present alongside Might, your AP floor is much higher than “gear AP.” That pushes many comparisons toward Strength and AP because those stats are scaling an already high baseline, rather than trying to compensate for a low one.
  • Hunter Party
    Effect: Adds Attack Power via an aura.
    Why it matters: It is a quiet baseline increase that makes your entire physical kit feel better. If your party has this aura, you are closer to the “supported melee group” scenario where physical scaling stats dominate and reliability stats gain more value.
  • Enhancement Party
    Effect: Percent Attack Power increase for your party tied to your crit engine and uptime.
    Why it matters: This is a multiplier on the party’s Attack Power. The more Attack Power the party has from gear plus buffs, the bigger the real output swing. It also means your reliability matters twice: it improves your damage and helps keep this multiplier active.
Gearing takeaway: In an AP supported party, Strength and Attack Power are the most stable scalers, and hit and expertise become more valuable because they protect high value swing events.

Crit and haste throughput

These increase the number of valuable swing events and amplify burst windows
  • Druid Party
    Effect: Increases crit chance for your group.
    Why it matters: Crit is your uptime engine. More crit means more Flurry uptime, smoother damage, and fewer “dead stretches” where your swing speed falls off. It also increases how often Unleashed Rage stays active in practice.
  • Warrior Party
    Effect: Increases group crit chance through an upkeep style buff.
    Why it matters: The practical outcome is the same: more Flurry stability and more reliable burst. When crit support is present, crit rating and agility upgrades tend to compare better because they reinforce a buffed uptime engine.
  • Balance Party
    Effect: Increases spell crit chance for your group.
    Why it matters: Enhancement is mostly physical, but shocks and spell based procs are still real throughput. More spell crit increases the payoff of your spell portion and makes “shock heavy” rotations slightly stronger. It also increases the consistency of Elemental Devastation uptime if you are playing into spell crit triggers.
  • Item Roster
    Effect: Increases the effect of for the Balance Druid wearing it.
    Why it matters: This is a raid composition modifier. If your raid has a Moonkin and they are wearing this idol, your party’s spell crit support is higher than the “default aura” assumption. That can shift close choices toward spell friendly stats or reinforce shock value on certain fights.
  • Shaman Party
    Effect: Large haste window that increases attack speed and casting speed.
    Why it matters: Haste increases attempts. If you are missing or getting dodged, haste creates more failed swing events. This is why hit and expertise become more valuable during Lust style windows: they convert extra attempts into actual damage.
  • Leatherworking Party
    Effect: Short duration haste buff for the group.
    Why it matters: Treat it as a smaller haste window. If you are under hit or expertise targets, these windows feel worse because more of the extra swing attempts fail. If you are capped, they feel like clean multipliers.
Gearing takeaway: Burst windows amplify whatever your current setup does well. Reliability (hit and expertise) is what converts haste windows into real damage, and crit support stabilizes your uptime engine.

Sustain and mana support

These protect your rotation by preventing OOM, which protects real DPS
  • Paladin Raidwide
    Effect: Mana regeneration while active.
    Why it matters: If you have to stop shocking or stop using utility due to mana, your real DPS drops regardless of how good your gear is. Wisdom protects sustained output by protecting your ability to keep pressing buttons.
  • Shaman Party
    Effect: Party mana regeneration.
    Why it matters: This increases the time you can maintain your chosen rotation without throttling shocks. On long fights, mana support can turn “theoretical DPS” into “repeatable DPS.”
Gearing takeaway: If you ever go OOM, solve sustain first. Damage stats only matter when you can afford to keep using your rotation.

Raid debuffs on the target

These are on the boss, not you, but they change your damage. Think of them as multipliers applied after your stats.

Armor reduction

Lower armor means more of your physical damage becomes real damage
  • Warrior Target
    Effect: Reduces target armor through stacking applications.
    Why it matters: When armor is reduced, each point of weapon DPS, Strength, Attack Power, and physical crit produces more damage because less is absorbed. That increases the real value of physical scaling stats, and it increases the penalty of missed swings because each swing is worth more.
  • Rogue Target
    Effect: Alternative major armor reduction effect.
    Why it matters: The specific provider matters less than the coverage: is major armor reduction present and maintained. If yes, physical scaling stats pull ahead. If it falls off, your physical stats effectively lose value until it is restored.
  • Druid Target
    Effect: Reduces armor and prevents stealth style behavior.
    Why it matters: It is common coverage and stacks with major armor reduction, so it is often part of the “expected raid baseline.” When it is present, your physical package is slightly stronger across the board.
  • Warlock Target
    Effect: Reduces target armor via a curse.
    Why it matters: More armor reduction means more payoff from weapon upgrades and Strength heavy items. On physical heavy raids, this pushes value toward the “hit the boss more and harder” stats.
Gearing takeaway: Strong armor reduction coverage increases the value of your physical scalers. This is a major reason raid DPS feels better than solo testing.

Physical damage amplification

These make each landed physical event worth more
  • Warrior Target
    Effect: Increases physical damage the target takes.
    Why it matters: If the boss takes more physical damage, your white swings, Stormstrike, and physical procs all improve. It also increases the value of hit and expertise because preventing a miss or dodge saves a now higher value swing.
  • Rogue Target
    Effect: Alternative physical damage taken amplifier.
    Why it matters: Coverage is the key. If your raid maintains one of these effects, your physical scaling stats gain value and close comparisons often shift toward weapon damage and AP style upgrades.
  • Hunter Target
    Effect: Converts the hunter’s stats into bonus Attack Power for attackers.
    Why it matters: This raises your Attack Power baseline dynamically. When it is active, percent multipliers and physical amplifiers scale a larger AP pool, which can change tight gear decisions.
Gearing takeaway: Physical amplifiers raise the value of your entire physical kit. They do not change one stat in isolation. They increase the payoff of “land more swings and hit harder.”
Rule: When comparing two items, first confirm your raid coverage for caps and multipliers. If you gain or lose a major buff or debuff, treat your EP snapshot as changed and re evaluate close swaps.
Confirm

Sim and sanity check workflow

Treat sims as the final decision maker, but only after your inputs match reality. If a sim result looks wrong, it almost always means an assumption, toggle, or comparison method is wrong.

Inputs that must be true

  • Raid context
    Buffs, debuffs, consumes, and totems match the group you care about.
  • Rotation intent
    Shock usage, twisting, and uptime assumptions match how you actually play.
  • Weapon rules
    Main hand and off hand speeds and imbues match the swap you are testing.
  • Caps and thresholds
    Hit and Expertise are evaluated against the same target and same assumptions.
Sanity check rule: If you do not trust a result, change one variable at a time until the result makes sense. When it still does not make sense, the input set is wrong, not the sim.

What to sim when comparing stats

  1. Compare the whole set. Do not swap one item in a vacuum if it changes caps, weapon pairs, or set bonuses.
  2. Sim the decision you will actually make. If you would also change gems, enchants, or consumes, include that version too.
  3. Use the right fight style. Patchwerk can mislead you if your real fights have movement, target switches, or downtime.
